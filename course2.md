# How to Study Scripture Without Contradiction
## Course Content

## Course Title
**How to Study Scripture Without Contradiction**

## Subtitle
**A beginner course in reading the Bible consistently, contextually, and faithfully**

---

# Course Overview

This course teaches young believers how to study the Bible in a way that respects the unity of Scripture, honors context, and avoids the common mistakes that lead people to think the Bible contradicts itself.

It is designed to help students move from confusion, prooftext-driven thinking, and panic over difficult passages to confidence, discernment, and careful biblical reading.

This course is intended to follow **Foundations of Defending Torah** and serve as the next step in helping students become stable readers of Scripture.

---

# Course Goal

Equip students to read passages more carefully, identify common interpretation mistakes, and understand the Bible as one coherent revelation rather than a collection of conflicting voices.

---

# Target Audience

- Teens and young adults
- Beginner to lower-intermediate Bible students
- Students who know basic Bible content but lack study tools
- Students who have heard conflicting teachings
- Students who want to grow in discernment and confidence

---

# Course Length

- 5 weeks
- 2 sessions per week
- 10 sessions total

---

# Session Length

- 45–60 minutes of teaching
- 10–15 minutes of discussion or reflection
- Approximately 1 hour of homework per session

---

# Course Promise

Learn how to read the Bible in context, avoid common interpretation mistakes, and study Scripture as one consistent story.

---

# Student Transformation

## Before the Course
The student may:
- Read verses in isolation
- Feel overwhelmed by difficult passages
- Depend on slogans or familiar church language
- Assume the Bible contradicts itself in places
- Struggle to compare passages carefully
- Feel unprepared to test what they are taught

## After the Course
The student should be able to:
- Explain why context matters
- Identify common Bible study mistakes
- Distinguish between clear passages and difficult passages
- Compare Scripture with Scripture responsibly
- Recognize how assumptions affect interpretation
- Study difficult passages without panic
- Read the Bible as one unified revelation

---

# Course Pillars

These ideas should be repeated throughout the course:

1. **Scripture is unified.**
2. **Context controls meaning.**
3. **Clear passages help interpret difficult passages.**
4. **Words must be understood in context.**
5. **Tradition must be tested by Scripture.**
6. **Good interpretation requires humility and patience.**

---

# Course Structure

## Week 1 — Why People Think the Bible Contradicts Itself

### Session 1 — The Bible Is One Story
**Main Goal:** Establish that Scripture should be read as a unified revelation.

**Big Idea:** The Bible is not a collection of disconnected religious opinions; it tells one coherent story.

**Key Questions:**
- Why do people think the Bible contradicts itself?
- How do the Torah, Prophets, Gospels, and Apostolic Writings fit together?
- Why should later passages not be read against earlier revelation?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- One God, one redemptive story
- Continuity of revelation
- Later Scripture builds on earlier Scripture
- Scripture helps interpret Scripture

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- Old Testament versus New Testament
- Jesus versus Moses
- Paul versus Torah

**Student Takeaway:**
“I should read the Bible as one story, not as competing voices.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Write a paragraph explaining how the Bible is unified
- Identify three common “Bible contradiction” claims you have heard
- Read a short set of passages showing continuity

---

### Session 2 — Why Context Changes Everything
**Main Goal:** Show students how badly verses can be misunderstood without context.

**Big Idea:** A verse cannot mean what its context does not allow it to mean.

**Key Questions:**
- What is context?
- Why do people misuse verses?
- What happens when context is ignored?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Immediate context
- Chapter context
- Book context
- Audience and situation
- Argument flow

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- “I found a verse, so I found the meaning.”
- Isolated verses settle everything
- Familiar phrases mean what people assume they mean

**Student Takeaway:**
“If I ignore context, I can make the Bible say almost anything.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Read one short passage and summarize its context
- Compare an isolated verse with the surrounding verses
- Answer: Why is context necessary?

---

## Week 2 — Learning How to Read Carefully

### Session 3 — Words Matter, But Context Matters More
**Main Goal:** Teach students not to misuse word studies.

**Big Idea:** Biblical words are important, but words do not carry the exact same meaning in every passage.

**Key Questions:**
- How should we study words?
- What mistakes do people make with word studies?
- Does one Greek or Hebrew meaning settle the whole issue?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Words have ranges of meaning
- Context determines usage
- Word studies can help, but they can also be abused
- Definitions alone do not settle doctrine

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- One lexicon definition solves everything
- One use of a word controls every use
- Language studies automatically beat context

**Student Takeaway:**
“I should use word studies carefully and never above context.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Compare the word “law” or “world” in different passages
- Identify where the same word seems to mean different things
- Explain why context matters more than a dictionary entry

---

### Session 4 — Clear Passages and Difficult Passages
**Main Goal:** Give students a method for handling hard texts without building doctrine on them first.

**Big Idea:** Difficult passages should be interpreted in harmony with clearer passages, not used to erase them.

**Key Questions:**
- What makes a passage difficult?
- Should unclear passages overturn clear ones?
- How do we study a hard text wisely?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Clear versus difficult passages
- Repeated themes versus isolated phrases
- Slow interpretation
- Humility in hard passages

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- The hardest verse wins
- One difficult text can erase dozens of clear ones
- Confusion means contradiction

**Student Takeaway:**
“I should let clear passages anchor my reading of difficult ones.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Identify one clear passage and one difficult one on a similar theme
- Write how the clear passage helps frame the difficult one
- Journal: Why do hard passages require patience?

---

## Week 3 — Interpreting the Bible Responsibly

### Session 5 — Let Scripture Interpret Scripture
**Main Goal:** Teach students how to compare related passages.

**Big Idea:** The best interpreter of Scripture is Scripture itself.

**Key Questions:**
- How do related passages help each other?
- When should we compare texts?
- How do we avoid comparing passages carelessly?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Thematic comparison
- Repeated biblical patterns
- Law, prophets, Messiah, and apostles in continuity
- Using cross-references wisely

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- Every verse stands alone
- Comparing texts is optional
- One passage can be understood without the rest of Scripture

**Student Takeaway:**
“I should compare passages carefully so I can understand the whole witness of Scripture.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Compare two passages on obedience, grace, covenant, or holiness
- Write what each adds to the bigger picture
- Practice building a small cross-reference chain

---

### Session 6 — Author, Audience, and Situation
**Main Goal:** Show that letters, prophecies, narratives, and teachings were written in real situations.

**Big Idea:** We understand a text better when we know who is speaking, to whom, and why.

**Key Questions:**
- Who wrote this?
- Who received it?
- What issue is being addressed?
- Why does that matter?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Historical setting
- Pastoral problems
- Disputes and correction
- Different types of biblical writing

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- Every line applies the same way in every setting
- Background never matters
- Letters can be read as abstract theology only

**Student Takeaway:**
“I should ask who, to whom, and why before drawing conclusions.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Choose a short epistle section and identify author, audience, and issue
- Summarize why that setting matters
- Complete a short exercise on interpreting a command in context

---

## Week 4 — Common Interpretation Mistakes

### Session 7 — Reading Assumptions into the Text
**Main Goal:** Help students recognize how traditions and assumptions shape interpretation.

**Big Idea:** Many people do not read the Bible plainly; they read it through inherited categories.

**Key Questions:**
- What assumptions do people bring to Scripture?
- How do traditions shape interpretation?
- How can we test our assumptions?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Reading into the text versus reading from the text
- Inherited church language
- Doctrinal filters
- Asking, “Where does the text actually say that?”

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- Assumptions feel like plain Bible truth
- Familiar teaching equals biblical teaching
- Labels settle meaning

**Student Takeaway:**
“I need to test my assumptions instead of reading them into Scripture.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- List three assumptions people often bring to the text
- Examine whether each is clearly stated in Scripture
- Reflection: What assumptions have shaped me?

---

### Session 8 — Prooftexts, Slogans, and Shortcut Theology
**Main Goal:** Train students to resist shallow argument patterns.

**Big Idea:** Slogans are not the same as careful Bible study.

**Key Questions:**
- What is a prooftext?
- Why are slogans persuasive?
- How do we move from slogan-thinking to biblical reasoning?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Incomplete quotations
- Theological shortcuts
- Repeated phrases without context
- Asking for full passage meaning

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- “The verse says it, that settles it.”
- A slogan explains a doctrine
- Confidence equals correctness

**Student Takeaway:**
“I should test slogans and prooftexts by reading the actual passage.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Take three common phrases and examine their context
- Rewrite a slogan into a fuller biblical explanation
- Identify one example of shortcut theology

---

## Week 5 — Building a Lifelong Study Habit

### Session 9 — A Simple Method for Studying Any Passage
**Main Goal:** Give students a repeatable beginner-friendly Bible study process.

**Big Idea:** Good Bible study can be simple, repeatable, and faithful.

**Key Questions:**
- What steps should I take when I study a passage?
- How do I avoid rushing?
- What questions should I always ask?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Observe
- Define
- Locate context
- Compare
- Interpret
- Apply
- Test conclusions with the wider witness of Scripture

**Suggested Beginner Study Method:**
1. What does the passage say?
2. What is the context?
3. Who is speaking and to whom?
4. What problem or issue is being addressed?
5. Are there related passages?
6. Does my interpretation fit the rest of Scripture?
7. How should I respond?

**Student Takeaway:**
“I now have a basic method I can use for studying Scripture on my own.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Apply the method to one short passage
- Fill out a study worksheet
- Summarize the passage in plain language

---

### Session 10 — How to Stay Grounded and Keep Growing
**Main Goal:** Help students develop long-term discernment and humility.

**Big Idea:** Good Bible study requires patience, humility, prayer, and a willingness to be corrected by Scripture.

**Key Questions:**
- How do I keep growing after this course?
- What habits keep me grounded?
- How do I avoid pride or confusion?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Humility in interpretation
- Prayerful study
- Patience with unresolved questions
- Testing teachers
- Staying anchored in Scripture
- Living what we learn

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- Knowing method means I now know everything
- Being critical equals being discerning
- Study without obedience is maturity

**Student Takeaway:**
“Faithful Bible study is a lifelong discipline of humility, patience, and obedience.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Create a personal study plan
- Write a reflection on how your approach to Scripture changed
- Final exercise: explain how to study the Bible without contradiction

- Delight in God's word
- Wisdom and understanding
- Testing teaching
- Rightly handling truth
- Meditation on the Word

# Assessment Approach

Assessment should feel encouraging and practical.

## Weekly Assessment Ideas
- Short reflection prompt
- Explain the big idea in your own words
- Identify one interpretation mistake
- Answer one question about context
- Recite and explain the memory verse

Choose one:
- A short written explanation of how to study the Bible without contradiction
- A 3–5 minute recorded explanation of a simple study method
- A completed study worksheet on a selected passage
- A personal statement on how the student's approach to Scripture has changed
