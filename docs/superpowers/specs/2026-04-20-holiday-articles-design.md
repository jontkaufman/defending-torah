**Date:** 2026-04-20

**Scope:** 5 comprehensive feast articles with objection-first apologetic approach
**Total Word Count:** ~22,000-24,000 words across all articles

## Executive Summary

This specification outlines the creation of 5 comprehensive articles covering all seven biblical feasts (Passover, Unleavened Bread, First Fruits, Pentecost, Trumpets, Day of Atonement, Tabernacles) for the Defending Torah (defend) Torah apologetics website.

**Core Mission:** Convince skeptical Christians that biblical feasts remain binding and should be observed, using an objection-first reversal structure that leads with apostolic practice evidence and systematically dismantles the "fulfilled = abolished" argument.

**Strategic Grouping:**
1. Passover and Unleavened Bread (combined — 8-day observance)
2. First Fruits and Pentecost (combined — connected by counting of Omer)
3. Feast of Trumpets (standalone)
4. Day of Atonement (standalone)
5. Feast of Tabernacles (standalone)

**Difficulty Mix:** 2 entry-level articles, 3-4 intermediate articles

**Primary Objection:** "Jesus fulfilled the feasts, so we don't observe them" (70% of content focused on dismantling this)

## 1. Article Structure Template

This template applies to all 5 feast articles, with depth adjustments per feast difficulty level.

### Section Breakdown

**1. The Objection Stated** (5% of article, ~200-300 words)
- Present the strongest form of "fulfilled = don't keep" objection
- Quote real sources where possible (study Bibles, popular teachers)
- No straw man — make it compelling
- Example opening: *"Every Easter, millions of Christians celebrate Christ's resurrection—but almost none observe Passover, the feast Jesus actually fulfilled. The reason? A single assumption that has shaped 2,000 years of Christian practice: 'Jesus fulfilled the feasts, so we don't keep them anymore.'"*

**2. The Smoking Gun** (15%, ~600-900 words)
- **Immediate counter-evidence** that creates cognitive dissonance
- For Passover: 1 Corinthians 5:7-8 ("Therefore let us keep the feast")
- For others: Acts 20:16 (Paul hastening to Jerusalem for Pentecost), apostolic practice
- Format: Bold the smoking gun text, then unpack: "Notice what Paul does NOT say..."
- Goal: Make reader think "Wait, I've never noticed that before"

**3. Why This Matters** (10%, ~400-600 words)
- Stakes: What's at risk if we're wrong about this?
- Three angles:
  - **Theological**: Connection to biblical calendar, God's prophetic timeline
  - **Obedience**: Matthew 5:19 (least/greatest in kingdom)
  - **Witness**: Living out whole-Bible faith, not just NT excerpt
- Tone: Urgent but not condemning

**4. Biblical Foundation** (20%, ~800-1,200 words)
- Now that reader is engaged, show what the feast actually is
- Torah commands (Leviticus 23, Deuteronomy 16)
- Historical observances (wilderness, Temple, exilic return)
- Symbolism (what it commemorates, what it teaches)
- Keep accessible — Hebrew terms defined on first use

**5. Fulfillment Rightly Understood** (25%, ~1,000-1,500 words)
- **The core apologetic section**
- How Yeshua fulfills the feast (timing, typology, NT witness)
- Critical distinction: **Fulfillment = completion, not termination**
- Matthew 5:17-19 ("not to abolish but to fulfill")
- Shadow/substance both remain (Colossians 2:17 proper reading)
- Use comparison table where helpful (Passover lamb → Yeshua parallels)

**6. Dismantling the Objection** (20%, ~800-1,200 words)
- Systematic counter-arguments to "fulfilled = abolished"
- **Primary arguments** (must include all):
  1. **Paul's practice**: He kept feasts AFTER resurrection (Acts 20:6, 16; 1 Cor 16:8)
  2. **Paul's logic**: 1 Cor 5:7-8 moves FROM Yeshua's sacrifice TO continued observance
  3. **Logical contradiction**: If "fulfilled = don't keep," why celebrate resurrection/communion?
  4. **Zechariah 14:16-19**: Feasts commanded in Millennium (use this HEAVILY for Tabernacles)
  5. **"Forever" language**: Leviticus 23 uses "statute forever throughout your generations"
- **Secondary responses** (brief, 1-2 paragraphs each):
  - "That's legalism" → Justification vs. sanctification distinction
  - Romans 14 → "Disputable matters" scope limiter
  - Colossians 2:16-17 → Corrected translation (mistranslation article link)
  - "No Temple" → Incomplete but faithful framework, Egypt precedent

**7. Observance Today** (5%, ~200-400 words)
- Practical, concise guide
- "What we CAN do" / "What we CANNOT do" (Temple-specific)
- Emphasize "incomplete but faithful" principle
- 3-5 concrete steps
- Tone: Invitational, not burdensome
- Example: "Determine the date, prepare the elements, gather your family, tell the story"

### Difficulty Level Adjustments

**Entry-Level Articles** (Trumpets, Atonement):
- Shorter overall (~3,500-4,000 words)
- Less Hebrew/Greek
- Simpler theological concepts
- Fewer sources cited

**Intermediate Articles** (Passover/Unleavened Bread, First Fruits/Pentecost, Tabernacles):
- Longer (~4,500-5,500 words)
- Moderate Hebrew/Greek (defined on first use)
- Deeper exegesis
- More extensive source engagement

**Title:** "Passover and Unleavened Bread: Why Christians Should Observe Both"

**Category:** arguments

**Unique Angles:**

- **Connection to Communion** — restoring the Lord's Supper to its Passover context
- **Easter replacement issue** — Why celebrate pagan-influenced Easter instead of biblical Passover?
- **Paul's explicit command** — 1 Cor 5:7-8 is the smoking gun for this article

**Key Smoking Gun:**
"Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed. **Therefore let us keep the feast**" (1 Cor 5:7-8)

**Special Emphases:**
- **Leaven symbolism** — Sin/corruption vs. sincerity/truth (bridges both feasts)

- **Yeshua's timing** — Crucified on Passover, buried during Unleavened Bread (prophetic precision)
- **Acts 20:6** — Paul marking time by "days of Unleavened Bread" years after resurrection

**Unique Objection Responses:**
- "We have Communion now" → Communion IS Passover, rightly understood; restore biblical context
- "Easter commemorates resurrection" → Resurrection is First Fruits, not Easter; wrong calendar

### Article 2: First Fruits and Pentecost

**Title:** "First Fruits and Pentecost: The Spring Harvest Feasts"
**Slug:** `first-fruits-and-pentecost-spring-harvest-feasts`
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Target Length:** 4,500-5,000 words
**Category:** arguments
**Tags:** first-fruits, pentecost, shavuot, resurrection, holy-spirit, counting-omer, acts-2

**Unique Angles:**
- **The 50-day connection** — Counting of the Omer links First Fruits → Pentecost
- **Resurrection on First Fruits** — Yeshua rose "on the day after the Sabbath" (the feast day)
- **Spirit outpouring on Pentecost** — Acts 2 happened ON the appointed time, not randomly
- **Barley → Wheat harvest sequence** — Agricultural typology (Yeshua = barley firstfruits, Church = wheat firstfruits)

**Key Smoking Guns:**
- **First Fruits:** "Christ has been raised from the dead, the firstfruits of those who have fallen asleep" (1 Cor 15:20) + John 20:17 (wave sheaf presentation?)
- **Pentecost:** Acts 20:16 ("Paul was hastening to be at Jerusalem, if possible, on the day of Pentecost") + 1 Cor 16:8 ("I will stay in Ephesus until Pentecost")

**Special Emphases:**
- **Spring feasts fulfilled "to the day"** — God's redemptive timeline follows the feast calendar
- **The two leavened loaves** (Pentecost) — Jew + Gentile, or Church still contains sin
- **Sinai connection** — Torah given at Pentecost (Exodus 19:1), Spirit given at Pentecost (Acts 2) — law written on hearts
- **Zechariah 14 doesn't mention First Fruits/Pentecost** in Millennium, but mentions Tabernacles — why? Because spring feasts already fulfilled in first coming

**Unique Objection Responses:**
- "The Spirit replaced the law" → No, the Spirit empowers obedience to law (Ezek 36:27, Rom 8:3-4)
- "Pentecost Sunday in church calendar" → Disconnected from biblical calendar, floating date vs. 50-day count

## 3. Content Guidelines

These standards apply across all 5 feast articles, drawn from the defend/CLAUDE.md research standards.

### Tone & Voice

**Principle:** Intellectually confident, not combative. Investigating, not prosecuting.

**Application:**
- **When presenting objections:** Respectful acknowledgment. "Many sincere Christians believe..." not "Ignorant church-goers think..."
- **When countering objections:** Firm but gracious. "The text actually says..." not "Anyone who claims X is blind to..."
- **When showing apostolic practice:** Let the evidence speak. "Notice what Paul does in Acts 20:6..." not "Paul obliterates this objection by..."
- **When calling to observance:** Invitational, not guilt-inducing. "Consider joining..." not "If you're disobedient, you'll..."

### Language & Terminology

**From CLAUDE.md standards:**

| **Use** | **Avoid** | **When/Why** |
|---------|-----------|--------------|
| Yeshua | Jesus | In theological contexts (restores 1st-century Jewish identity) |
| Torah | The Law | "Law" implies legal code; Torah = instruction |
| Apostolic writings | New Testament | When emphasizing continuation, not replacement |
| Tanakh | Old Testament | Avoids supersessionist framing |
| Pesach, Shavuot, Sukkot | Passover, Pentecost, Tabernacles | Introduce Hebrew on first use, then alternate |
| Mitzvot | Commandments | When emphasizing covenantal register |

**Application Rules:**
1. **First use:** Introduce Hebrew with transliteration + English + definition
   - Example: "Passover (Hebrew: פֶּסַח, *Pesach*, 'to pass over')"
2. **Subsequent uses:** Alternate between English and Hebrew, preferring English for readability
3. **Entry-level articles:** Less Hebrew, more English
4. **Intermediate articles:** Moderate Hebrew, defined on first use
5. **Greek terms:** Use when exegetically necessary (σκηνόω in John 1:14, τέλος in Romans 10:4)

### Evidence & Citation Standards

**Primary Sources (Always Cite):**
- **Torah commands:** Chapter:verse, include brief quote
- **NT practice:** Acts, Paul's letters, Gospels
- **Prophetic texts:** Zechariah 14, Isaiah 27, etc.

**Citation Format:**
- Inline for Scripture: "As Paul writes in 1 Corinthians 5:7-8..."
- Footnote style for secondary sources: Brief inline mention, longer citation in parenthetical
- Link to wiki articles for deeper dives

### Steelman Requirement

**From CLAUDE.md:** "Every essay on a contested topic must state the **strongest** opposing argument (scholarly form, not straw man)."

**Application:**
- **Section 1 (The Objection Stated):** Present the best form of "fulfilled = abolished"
- Quote theologians if possible (ESV Study Bible, commentators)
- Acknowledge its appeal: "This view has shaped Christian practice for 2,000 years..."
- **Never oversimplify:** Don't reduce objections to absurdity before addressing them

### Confidence Levels

**From CLAUDE.md:** Use confidence markers where appropriate.

**Application:**
- **[Established]:** Paul kept feasts after resurrection (Acts 20:6, 16; 1 Cor 16:8) — textual fact
- **[Probable]:** Yeshua born during Tabernacles — strong circumstantial
- **[Working hypothesis]:** "Day no one knows" = Trumpets new moon sighting — plausible
- **[Open question]:** Timing of First Fruits (16th Nisan vs. Sunday) — genuinely debated
- **[Disputed]:** Acts 15 scope (justification vs. sanctification) — scholarly debate

### Inconvenient Evidence Policy

**From CLAUDE.md:** "If evidence complicates the thesis: **include it**. Note it, respond to it, or mark it open. Never bury it."

**Application:**
- **Colossians 2:16-17:** Most translations read as abolishing feasts. Address it head-on.
- **Acts 15:** Doesn't explicitly command Gentiles to keep feasts. Respond with v.21 + Paul's practice.
- **No explicit NT command:** "Observe Passover" isn't commanded like "Love one another." Note this, respond with apostolic practice + Matthew 5:17-19.

### Hebrew/Greek Usage

**When to Use:**
- **Hebrew:** When semantic range matters (Pesach, teruah, sukkot)
- **Greek:** When NT exegesis requires it (τέλος, σκηνόω, πληρόω)

**When NOT to Use:**
- Don't decorate with unnecessary Hebrew/Greek
- Don't use without defining on first use
- Don't assume reader knows Hebrew alphabet (always transliterate)

**Format:**
"The Hebrew word *teruah* (תְּרוּעָה) means 'shout,' 'alarm,' or 'trumpet blast,' carrying connotations of urgency and awakening."
