# Yeshua, Paul, and the Torah Question
## Course Content

## Course Title
**Yeshua, Paul, and the Torah Question**

## Subtitle
**A beginner course on the New Testament passages most often used against Torah**

---

# Course Overview

This course helps young believers understand the New Testament passages most often used against Torah by reading Yeshua and Paul in context and in continuity with the rest of Scripture.

It is designed to help students move from fear, confusion, and dependence on slogans to confidence, clarity, and a more stable understanding of biblical doctrine.

This course is intended to follow **Foundations of Defending Torah** and **How to Study Scripture Without Contradiction**.

---

# Course Goal

Equip students to approach the main anti-Torah New Testament passages with clarity, explain common misunderstandings, and understand Yeshua and Paul within the consistent story of Scripture.

---

# Target Audience

- Teens and young adults
- Beginner to lower-intermediate Bible students
- Students who are confused by New Testament passages about law and grace
- Students who want to understand Paul more carefully
- Students who want a stronger foundation for defending Torah and biblical doctrine

---

# Course Length

- 5 weeks
- 2 sessions per week
- 10 sessions total

---

# Session Length

- 45–60 minutes of teaching
- 10–15 minutes of discussion or reflection
- Approximately 1 hour of homework per session

---

# Course Promise

Learn how to understand the New Testament passages most often used against Torah by reading Yeshua and Paul in context and in continuity with the rest of Scripture.

---

# Student Transformation

## Before the Course
The student may:
- Feel shaken by Paul’s letters
- Assume hard New Testament passages overturn Torah
- Be confused by phrases like “under the law” or “fulfilled”
- Depend on church slogans or isolated prooftexts
- Struggle to answer objections related to grace, works, and obedience
- Feel unsure whether Yeshua and Paul agree with Moses and the prophets

## After the Course
The student should be able to:
- Explain why Yeshua did not abolish Torah
- Understand “fulfill” more carefully
- Explain that grace is not lawlessness
- Recognize major misunderstandings in Paul’s letters
- Distinguish between justification and obedience
- Understand how Jew-Gentile tensions affect many New Testament arguments
- Respond to common anti-Torah prooftexts with more clarity and confidence

---

# Course Pillars

These ideas should be repeated throughout the course:

1. **Yeshua does not contradict the Father.**
2. **The New Testament must be read in continuity with earlier Scripture.**
3. **Grace does not erase holiness.**
4. **Paul opposes misuse of Torah, not God’s Torah itself.**
5. **Justification and obedience are not the same issue.**
6. **Context is essential for hard passages.**

---

# Course Structure

## Week 1 — Yeshua and the Law

### Session 1 — Did Yeshua Abolish the Torah?
**Main Goal:** Establish clearly that Yeshua did not come to destroy the Law or Prophets.

**Big Idea:** Yeshua upheld the Father’s instruction and taught it rightly.

**Key Questions:**
- What did Yeshua actually say about the Law and the Prophets?
- What does it mean that He came not to destroy but to fulfill?
- Did Yeshua oppose Torah, or did He oppose false teaching?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Yeshua and continuity
- Law and Prophets language
- Fulfilling versus abolishing
- Tradition versus commandment
- Messiah as teacher of true obedience

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- “Fulfill means end.”
- “Jesus replaced Moses.”
- “Jesus taught love instead of commandments.”

**Student Takeaway:**
“Yeshua did not abolish Torah; He upheld and clarified it.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Summarize what Yeshua taught about commandments
- Compare one passage on commandment-keeping with one on love
- Explain in plain language why “fulfill” does not automatically mean “abolish”

---

### Session 2 — Love, Obedience, and the Commandments
**Main Goal:** Show that love and obedience belong together.

**Big Idea:** Biblical love does not replace God’s commandments; it expresses them.

**Key Questions:**
- Does love replace law?
- What does it mean to love God biblically?
- How are love and obedience connected?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Love of God
- Love of neighbor
- Commandments as the shape of love
- Inward and outward faithfulness

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- “Love makes commandments unnecessary.”
- “Jesus simplified religion by removing commands.”
- “As long as your heart is right, specific obedience is secondary.”

**Student Takeaway:**
“Love and obedience are not opposites in Scripture.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Identify passages that connect love and obedience
- Write a short response to “love replaces the law”
- Reflection: Why does biblical love need definition?

---

## Week 2 — Paul, Grace, and Justification

### Session 3 — What Does Paul Mean by “Under the Law”?
**Main Goal:** Help students see that Paul’s language must be read contextually.

**Big Idea:** “Under the law” does not simply mean living in obedience to God.

**Key Questions:**
- What does Paul mean by this phrase?
- Is he speaking about obedience, condemnation, covenant status, or misuse?
- Why does this phrase confuse so many people?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Context in Paul
- Law in different senses
- Condemnation and sin
- The danger of flattening Paul’s vocabulary

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- “Not under law means commandments do not matter.”
- “Grace and law are opposites.”
- “Paul rejected obedient living.”

**Student Takeaway:**
“I can explain that ‘not under law’ does not mean freedom from holiness.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Paraphrase the phrase in context
- Compare two Pauline uses of “law”
- Answer: Why is context necessary in Paul?

---

### Session 4 — Faith, Works, and Justification
**Main Goal:** Distinguish clearly between earning righteousness and walking in obedience.

**Big Idea:** We are not justified by works of law, but that does not make obedience irrelevant.

**Key Questions:**
- What is justification?
- What are “works of the law” debates about?
- Why do people confuse justification and obedience?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Justification
- Faith
- Obedience
- Legalism
- Covenant faithfulness
- Earning versus responding

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- “If obedience matters, that means salvation by works.”
- “Faith excludes action.”
- “Any serious call to obey is legalism.”

**Student Takeaway:**
“I can distinguish between being justified and walking in obedience.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Define justification, obedience, and legalism in plain words
- Compare passages on faith and obedience
- Write a short answer to “Torah-keeping means trying to earn salvation”

---

## Week 3 — The Hard Pauline Passages

### Session 5 — Galatians: What Problem Is Paul Actually Addressing?
**Main Goal:** Show that Galatians is often misread because students do not know the issue Paul is confronting.

**Big Idea:** Galatians argues against a distorted use of law in relation to justification and covenant identity, not against God’s instruction itself.

**Key Questions:**
- What problem is happening in Galatia?
- Why is circumcision such a central issue?
- Is Paul attacking Torah, or a misuse of Torah?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Historical controversy
- Circumcision and identity
- Justification by faith
- Coercion and false confidence
- Reading arguments in context

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- “Galatians proves the law is over.”
- “Paul says the law is bad.”
- “Circumcision debates abolish Torah.”

**Student Takeaway:**
“Galatians addresses a misuse of Torah, not the goodness of Torah itself.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Summarize the Galatian controversy
- Explain why context matters in Galatians
- Answer one objection based on a common Galatians prooftext

---

### Session 6 — Romans: Is Paul Against the Law?
**Main Goal:** Help students read Romans more carefully and avoid oversimplifying it.

**Big Idea:** Romans does not present the law as the enemy; it presents sin as the enemy.

**Key Questions:**
- What does Romans say about the law?
- How does Paul speak positively and negatively about law in different contexts?
- What is the relationship between grace, sin, and obedience?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Law and sin
- Holy, righteous, and good
- Flesh and spirit
- Grace and dominion
- Obedience from the heart

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- “Romans teaches that law is bondage.”
- “Grace means obedience no longer matters.”
- “Paul says the law is the problem.”

**Student Takeaway:**
“In Romans, the problem is sin and flesh, not God’s law.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Compare a positive statement about the law with a difficult one
- Write a short summary of Romans on law and sin
- Explain why grace does not mean moral freedom from God

---

## Week 4 — Common Prooftexts Against Torah

### Session 7 — “The Law Was Nailed to the Cross”
**Main Goal:** Teach students to separate the canceling of debt from the abolishing of God’s standards.

**Big Idea:** Messiah’s death removes guilt and condemnation, not the definition of righteousness.

**Key Questions:**
- What was canceled?
- What do debt and condemnation language mean?
- Why do people assume the cross abolished the commandments?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Debt language
- Forgiveness
- Condemnation
- Sin and law
- Holiness after the cross

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- “The cross ended the law.”
- “If sin is forgiven, commandments are unnecessary.”
- “Grace makes holiness optional.”

**Student Takeaway:**
“I can explain the difference between canceling debt and canceling God’s commandments.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Chart sin, law, grace, forgiveness, and obedience
- Answer three prooftext statements simply
- Reflection: Why should forgiveness deepen gratitude and obedience?

---

### Session 8 — Acts 15, Colossians, and Other Frequently Used Objections
**Main Goal:** Give students a framework for several key passages that are regularly used against Torah.

**Big Idea:** Difficult prooftexts must be read in context rather than treated as isolated anti-Torah slogans.

**Key Questions:**
- What is Acts 15 actually deciding?
- What is happening in Colossians?
- How should students handle passages they do not fully understand yet?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Jew-Gentile table issues
- Entry requirements versus discipleship growth
- Warnings about judgment
- Shadow language
- Patience with complex passages

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- “Acts 15 limits Gentiles to four rules forever.”
- “Colossians clearly abolishes Sabbath and food laws.”
- “One difficult verse settles everything.”

**Student Takeaway:**
“I can approach commonly quoted anti-Torah passages without panic or oversimplification.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Choose one passage and explain its likely context
- Write a short response to a common objection from Acts 15 or Colossians
- List what questions to ask before drawing conclusions from a difficult text

---

## Week 5 — A Consistent New Testament Framework

### Session 9 — How to Read the New Testament Without Contradicting the Old
**Main Goal:** Help students build a stable overall framework for biblical continuity.

**Big Idea:** The New Testament must be read as the continuation of the same story, not the cancellation of it.

**Key Questions:**
- How should we relate the Testaments?
- What happens when later writings are used against earlier revelation?
- What principles keep our reading consistent?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Continuity of revelation
- Covenant fulfillment without contradiction
- Messiah in Israel’s story
- Apostles in continuity with the prophets and Torah

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- “New always means replacement.”
- “The apostles moved beyond Torah.”
- “The Old Testament is background, not authority.”

**Student Takeaway:**
“I should read the New Testament as the continuation of God’s consistent revelation.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Write a one-page summary of continuity between Torah, prophets, Yeshua, and Paul
- Identify three principles for reading both Testaments together
- Explain why contradiction is the wrong starting assumption

---

### Session 10 — Answering New Testament Objections with Wisdom and Confidence
**Main Goal:** Prepare students to explain these issues clearly and calmly.

**Big Idea:** Students should be able to respond to common objections without fear, arrogance, or confusion.

**Key Questions:**
- How do I explain hard passages simply?
- What should I do when I do not know the full answer?
- How do I respond faithfully and patiently?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Clear explanation
- Asking questions
- Staying calm
- Avoiding overstatement
- Grounding arguments in major themes
- Humility in unresolved issues

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- “I must know everything before I speak.”
- “Strong doctrine requires a harsh tone.”
- “If I cannot answer every detail, I have failed.”

**Student Takeaway:**
“I can respond to New Testament Torah objections with more clarity, peace, and confidence.”

**Homework Ideas:**
- Prepare short answers to three common objections
- Record or write a three-minute explanation of one difficult passage
- Final reflection: What changed in the way I read the New Testament?

# Memory Verse Strategy

Use one memory verse per week.

## Memory Verse Goals
- Reinforce the unity of Scripture
- Strengthen confidence in Yeshua and the commandments
- Clarify the relationship between grace and obedience
- Help students remember key doctrinal truths
- Encourage meditation on the Word

## Recommended Memory Verse Themes
- Yeshua and the commandments
- Grace and obedience
- The goodness of God’s law
- Warnings against lawlessness
- Faith and faithfulness
- Harmony within Scripture

- Summarize one hard passage simply
