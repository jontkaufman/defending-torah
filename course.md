# Foundations of Defending Torah
## Course Content Specification

## Purpose of This File
This file defines the structure, scope, tone, and content plan for a beginner-level course for DefendingTorah.com.

This is **not** a full lesson manuscript.  
This is a **course blueprint** for developing the lessons, homework, study guides, quizzes, and supporting materials.

The course is intended to help build a 4–6 week beginner training course for young people who want to understand and defend Torah and biblical doctrine in a simple, faithful, Scripture-centered way.

---

# Course Overview

## Course Title
**Foundations of Defending Torah**

## Subtitle
**A beginner course in biblical continuity, obedience, and answering objections**

## Course Goal
Equip young believers to understand Torah as part of God's unchanging instruction, recognize how Yeshua and the apostles fit within that story, and answer common objections with clarity, humility, and confidence.

## Target Audience
- Teens and young adults
- Beginners to lower-intermediate Bible students
- Not designed for seminary or college-level students
- Students may know basic Bible stories but lack confidence in doctrine
- Students may have heard common church objections to Torah and need a clear biblical framework

## Course Length
- 5 weeks
- 2 sessions per week
- 10 sessions total

## Session Length
- 45–60 minutes teaching
- 10–15 minutes discussion/reflection
- Approximately 1 hour of homework per session

## Course Type
- Bible foundations
- Beginner apologetics
- Discipleship-oriented doctrinal training

## Core Promise
This course helps students read the Bible as one consistent story, understand Torah in its biblical context, and answer common objections without becoming argumentative or proud.

---

# Student Transformation

## Before the Course
The student may:
- Be confused by "law vs grace"
- Think Torah means legalism or bondage
- Be unsure how the Old and New Testaments fit together
- Feel intimidated by passages from Paul
- Struggle to answer objections from friends, church leaders, or family
- Feel interested in Torah but lack a stable framework
- Be tempted to argue emotionally rather than respond biblically

## After the Course
The student should be able to:
- Explain Torah as God's instruction, not man-made legalism
- Show that Yeshua did not abolish the Father's commandments
- Recognize common misunderstandings about Paul
- Answer basic objections to Torah with simple biblical reasoning
- Study Scripture more carefully and contextually
- Defend biblical doctrine with humility, patience, and confidence

---

# Course Pillars

These ideas should be repeated throughout the course:

1. **God is consistent.**
2. **Torah is good.**
3. **Yeshua did not abolish the Father's commandments.**
4. **Paul must be read in context.**
5. **Grace and obedience are not enemies.**
6. **Truth should be defended with humility.**

---

# Tone and Teaching Style

## Tone Requirements
- Warm
- Confident
- Scripture-centered
- Patient
- Approachable
- Non-academic
- Clear and direct
- Never mocking or hostile

## Writing Level
- Roughly 8th–10th grade reading level
- Define important terms simply
- Use short paragraphs
- Use plain language before technical language

## Teaching Philosophy
- Simple before technical
- Clear before comprehensive
- Repeat core ideas often
- Build confidence slowly
- Focus on Scripture, not reactionary argument
- Train students to understand, not merely debate
- Emphasize both doctrine and character

## Tone Guardrails
Avoid:
- Academic overload
- Excessive theological jargon
- Mocking other Christians or traditions
- Angry or combative language
- Overdependence on niche controversies
- Treating students like experts
- Presenting the course as debate warfare

Preferred voice:
**Teach like a patient older brother or mentor, not like an internet debater.**

# Core Doctrinal Commitments of the Course

The course should consistently reinforce these convictions:

- Torah is God's instruction and reflects His holiness and wisdom
- God's character does not change
- Obedience is not legalism
- Yeshua upheld and properly taught Torah
- The new covenant does not create a Torah-free religion
- Grace does not mean lawlessness
- Paul does not contradict Yeshua, Moses, or the prophets
- Difficult passages must be interpreted in harmony with the wider witness of Scripture
- Believers should defend truth with humility and patience

---

# Main Objections the Course Should Address

The course should train students to answer these common objections:

1. "We are not under the law."
2. "Jesus fulfilled the law so we do not have to keep it."
3. "The law was nailed to the cross."
4. "The Sabbath was abolished."
5. "The law was only for Israel."
6. "Paul taught against the law."
7. "Grace means commandments no longer apply."
8. "Love replaces the law."
9. "The old covenant is gone, so the law is gone."
10. "Food laws are no longer relevant."
11. "Trying to obey Torah is legalism."
12. "Christians only need the New Testament."

# Standard Session Format

Each lesson should follow this consistent structure:

1. Opening question
2. Session big idea
3. Key Scripture readings
4. Main teaching points
5. Common misunderstanding or objection
6. Scriptural response
7. Real-life application
8. Discussion/reflection questions
9. Memory verse
10. Homework assignment

---

# Homework Philosophy

Homework should be simple, clear, and practical.

## Best Homework Types
- Short Scripture reading
- Verse comparison worksheet
- Define terms in your own words
- Answer one objection simply
- Reflection journal prompt
- Memory verse review
- Short response as if speaking to a friend
- Audio/video reflection
- Compare a difficult passage with a clearer passage

## Avoid
- Long academic essays
- Dense research assignments
- Heavy use of Hebrew/Greek for beginners
- Too many cross-references at once
- Work that feels like college theology

# Course Structure

## Week 1 - Foundations: One God, One Word, One Story

### Session 1 - What Is Torah?
**Main Goal:** Give students a clean, simple, biblical definition of Torah.

**Big Idea:** Torah is God's instruction, not a man-made burden.

**Key Questions:**
- What does Torah mean?
- Why do people misunderstand Torah?
- Why did God give commandments?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Torah as instruction, teaching, direction
- Torah reveals God's holiness, wisdom, and order
- Commandments are not the enemy
- Torah belongs within covenant relationship

**Common Misunderstandings to Address:**
- Torah equals legalism
- Torah is irrelevant today
- Torah is against love and grace

**Student Takeaway:**
"I can explain Torah as God's instruction for His people."

**Homework Ideas:**
- Read selected passages about delighting in God's commands
- Define Torah in one paragraph
- List three misconceptions people commonly have about Torah

---

### Session 2 - Does God Change?
**Main Goal:** Establish continuity in God's character and standards.

**Big Idea:** The God of Scripture is consistent, and His holiness does not change.

**Key Questions:**
- Does God change?
- Can something once righteous later become unrighteous?
- How do continuity and covenant development fit together?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- God's character is faithful and unchanging
- His standards flow from His character
- Faith and obedience are not opposites
- Development in covenant administration is not the same as contradiction

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- "That was the God of the Old Testament."
- "Jesus brought an entirely different religion."
- "Grace replaced obedience."

**Student Takeaway:**
"The Bible presents one consistent God, not two different systems."

**Homework Ideas:**
- Compare passages about God's character
- Journal response: Why would a loving God give instruction?
- Worksheet on continuity versus contradiction

---

## Week 2 - Yeshua and Torah

### Session 3 - What Did Yeshua Actually Teach?
**Main Goal:** Show that Yeshua upheld, clarified, and lived Torah faithfully.

**Big Idea:** Yeshua did not oppose God's commandments; He exposed false interpretations and hypocrisy.

**Key Questions:**
- Did Yeshua teach against Torah?
- What does "fulfill" mean?
- Who was He actually confronting?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Yeshua and the Law and Prophets
- "Fulfill" does not mean "cancel"
- Yeshua deepens righteous living
- Yeshua rebukes traditions that nullify God's commands

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- "Jesus came to end the law."
- "Love replaces commandments."
- "If Jesus criticized the Pharisees, He must have rejected Torah."

**Student Takeaway:**
"Yeshua upheld God's commandments and taught their true meaning."

**Homework Ideas:**
- Compare a passage where Yeshua corrects tradition
- Write five sentences on what "fulfill" does not mean
- Memory verse work

---

### Session 4 - Did Yeshua Start a New Religion?
**Main Goal:** Help students see Messiah in continuity with Israel, covenant, and prophecy.

**Big Idea:** The new covenant is renewed covenant faithfulness, not a new Torah-free religion.

**Key Questions:**
- Who is Yeshua in the story of Scripture?
- What is the new covenant?
- What becomes new, and what remains constant?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Messiah as fulfillment of prophetic hope
- Restoration, not abandonment
- The law written on the heart
- Inward transformation and covenant loyalty

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- "New covenant means old commandments are gone."
- "Jesus replaced Israel."
- "The New Testament starts over from scratch."

**Student Takeaway:**
"The new covenant deepens obedience through transformation; it does not make obedience irrelevant."

**Homework Ideas:**
- Compare wrong assumptions about the new covenant with biblical descriptions
- Answer: What is actually new in the new covenant?
- Create a simple timeline from Torah to prophets to Messiah

---

## Week 3 - Paul: The Most Misused Passages

### Session 5 - What Does "Not Under Law" Mean?
**Main Goal:** Give students a basic framework for reading Paul without panic.

**Big Idea:** Being "not under law" is not the same as being free from God's instruction.

**Key Questions:**
- What is Paul discussing in context?
- Does grace cancel obedience?
- Does Paul always use the word "law" in the same way?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Law can be used in different senses depending on context
- Condemnation versus obedience
- Grace frees from sin's dominion, not from holiness
- Justification is not earned through law-keeping, but obedience still matters

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- "Under grace means no commandments."
- "Paul and Jesus disagree."
- "Any effort to obey is legalism."

**Student Takeaway:**
"I can explain that grace does not mean lawlessness."

**Homework Ideas:**
- Paraphrase "not under law" in plain language
- Compare two verses often misunderstood
- Write one paragraph answering: Is obedience anti-grace?

---

### Session 6 - Did Paul Reject Torah?
**Main Goal:** Teach students to read Paul through the rest of Scripture instead of against it.

**Big Idea:** Paul opposes misuse of Torah, not God's Torah itself.

**Key Questions:**
- What problems was Paul addressing?
- Why do people read Paul as anti-Torah?
- How should difficult passages be handled?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Jew and Gentile tensions
- Justification debates
- Circumcision controversies
- Peter's warning that Paul can be hard to understand
- Hard passages should not overturn clearer passages

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- "Paul abolished the law."
- "Paul taught the opposite of Moses."
- "Acts and Paul's letters contradict each other."

**Student Takeaway:**
"Paul must be interpreted consistently with Scripture, not in contradiction to it."

**Homework Ideas:**
- Take one difficult Paul passage and summarize its likely context
- Define legalism, justification, and obedience simply
- Journal response: Why is context important?

---

## Week 4 - Common Objections

### Session 7 - "The Law Was Nailed to the Cross"
**Main Goal:** Teach students how to distinguish between the removal of sin's debt and the removal of God's standards.

**Big Idea:** Messiah's death removes guilt and condemnation, not the definition of holiness.

**Key Questions:**
- What was canceled?
- Was sin removed, or was righteousness redefined?
- How should these prooftexts be read carefully?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Debt and condemnation language
- Forgiveness versus abolition
- Law exposes sin; it is not itself the problem
- The cross and obedience belong together

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- "The cross ended the commandments."
- "If we are forgiven, obedience is optional."
- "Holiness commands were temporary and now meaningless."

**Student Takeaway:**
"I can explain the difference between canceling debt and canceling God's standards."

**Homework Ideas:**
- Create a chart: sin / law / grace / forgiveness / obedience
- Answer three objection statements in simple language
- Reflection: Why does forgiveness make obedience more meaningful?

---

### Session 8 - Sabbath, Food, and the "Old Testament Law" Label
**Main Goal:** Help students handle the most practical commandments people object to.

**Big Idea:** Many objections come from inherited categories rather than careful biblical reading.

**Key Questions:**
- Why is Sabbath often attacked?
- Are food laws only symbolic?
- Are moral/civil/ceremonial categories biblical or imported?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Sabbath as gift, sign, and holiness marker
- Food and holiness distinctions
- Caution with imposed theological categories
- Reading commandments in actual scriptural context

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- "Sabbath was only for Jews."
- "Jesus is our Sabbath, so the day no longer matters."
- "Food laws are obviously abolished."
- "Old Testament law is a simple category that can just be dismissed."

**Student Takeaway:**
"I can think more carefully about commandments people usually dismiss."

**Homework Ideas:**
- Choose one commandment and defend its continuing importance
- Prepare a short response to a Sabbath objection
- Complete a worksheet on assumptions versus actual text

---

## Week 5 - Defending Torah with Maturity

### Session 9 - How to Respond Without Pride
**Main Goal:** Train character and tone, not just arguments.

**Big Idea:** A biblical defense should sound like someone who loves truth and people.

**Key Questions:**
- How do we defend doctrine without becoming combative?
- What should we do when we do not know an answer?
- How do we disagree well?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Patience in discussion
- Asking questions before debating
- Avoiding arrogance
- Not overwhelming people
- Letting Scripture speak clearly

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- "Being bold means being aggressive."
- "If I win the argument, I have succeeded."
- "Everyone who disagrees is dishonest."

**Student Takeaway:**
"I can defend Torah in a way that reflects righteousness in both content and tone."

**Homework Ideas:**
- Roleplay responses to three common objections
- Write a gracious answer to a skeptical friend
- Self-check reflection: Where am I tempted toward pride?

---

### Session 10 - How to Keep Growing After the Course
**Main Goal:** Give students a pathway for lifelong biblical maturity.

**Big Idea:** This course gives foundations, not final mastery.

**Key Questions:**
- How should I keep studying?
- How do I avoid confusion or imbalance?
- What habits build long-term strength?

**Concepts to Teach:**
- Studying passages in context
- Comparing Scripture with Scripture
- Not building doctrine on slogans
- Obedience, prayer, and humility
- Learning in community without losing discernment

**Common Misunderstandings:**
- "Now I already know enough."
- "The goal is only to refute people."
- "Study without practice equals maturity."

**Student Takeaway:**
"I have a framework for continued study, obedience, and wise defense of biblical doctrine."

**Homework Ideas:**
- Final statement of belief
- Personal reflection: What changed in my thinking?
- Five-point defense of Torah for beginners

# Build Instructions for Future Lesson Development

When generating the actual lessons, keep the following rules:

1. Do not write at a college or seminary level.
2. Assume the student is new to doctrinal study.
3. Explain one major idea at a time.
4. Use a small number of key passages rather than overwhelming lists.
5. Include common objections and simple biblical responses.
6. Keep the tone respectful and calm.
7. Avoid attacking other traditions unnecessarily.
8. Keep the lessons devotional as well as doctrinal.
9. Reinforce the course pillars frequently.
10. End each lesson with a practical application and clear homework.
